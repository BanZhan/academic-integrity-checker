# Academic Misconduct Taxonomy

Use this taxonomy for pre-submission risk review. It is based on current publication-ethics guidance and proven misconduct cases, but it is a prevention checklist rather than a legal finding.

## Most Common Risk Types

1. Fabrication: invented data, patients, samples, experiments, survey responses, images, references, approvals, or reviewer identities.
2. Falsification: selective omission, manipulated images, relabeled experiments, altered numbers, undisclosed exclusions, p-hacking presented as confirmatory, or changing methods/results so the record is misleading.
3. Plagiarism and unattributed overlap: copied words, ideas, data, methods, figures, translations, or close paraphrase without credit.
4. Image and figure manipulation: duplicated panels, reused microscopy/blot images for different conditions, local brightness/contrast changes, splicing without markers, clone/heal edits, cropped-out contradictory information.
5. Authorship abuse: ghost authors, gift/guest authors, undisclosed writing assistance, author-order disputes, lack of final approval, or missing contributor accountability.
6. Duplicate or overlapping publication: salami slicing, self-plagiarism, undisclosed preprint/conference/thesis overlap, redundant submission to multiple journals.
7. Ethics and consent failures: missing IRB/ethics approval, missing informed consent, missing clinical trial registration, animal welfare gaps, unapproved secondary use of sensitive data.
8. Data/code availability and reproducibility gaps: no access statement, inaccessible repository, missing raw data for central claims, missing protocol, undocumented exclusions, unreported preprocessing.
9. Citation manipulation: fake references, citations that do not support claims, coercive citation, excessive self-citation, citation cartels, hidden or irrelevant references.
10. Peer-review and publication-process manipulation: fake reviewer identities, paper-mill manuscripts, purchased authorship, template-generated papers, fabricated reviewer reports, systematic manipulation of submissions.
11. AI-use problems: undisclosed AI-generated text/images/data/code where disclosure is required, hallucinated citations, unverified AI outputs, confidential data entered into prohibited systems.

## Minimum Submission-Ready Evidence

- All authors have reviewed the final manuscript and can identify their contributions.
- Raw data, analysis scripts, protocols, and figure source files are retained and can be audited.
- Ethics approval, consent, animal welfare, registration, and permission identifiers are present where relevant.
- Data/code/material availability is specific, not generic.
- Every reused element is credited and licensed/permissioned.
- Every image adjustment is legitimate, applied consistently, and disclosed when required.
- References are real, relevant, and support the claims made.
- AI assistance is disclosed if the journal, funder, institution, or field norm requires it.

## Source Notes

- The U.S. Office of Research Integrity defines research misconduct as fabrication, falsification, or plagiarism, excluding honest error and differences of opinion.
- ORI case summaries provide adjudicated examples of falsified/fabricated data, relabeled images, reused data, and fabricated patient or sample information.
- ICMJE Recommendations, updated January 2026, cover authorship, conflicts of interest, participant protection, overlapping publication, clinical trials, manuscript preparation, and AI use in publishing.
- COPE retraction guidance is widely used by journals for correcting the literature when findings are unreliable because of misconduct or major error.
